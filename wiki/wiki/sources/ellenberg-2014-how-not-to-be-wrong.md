# How Not to Be Wrong: The Power of Mathematical Thinking

**Authors:** Jordan Ellenberg · **Year:** 2014 · **Venue:** The Penguin Press · **Type:** book

## Summary

A popular-mathematics book arguing that mathematics is the formalization and disciplining of common sense - a 'science of not being wrong'. Organized into five themes (Linearity, Inference, Expectation, Regression, Existence), it uses case studies (Wald's missing bullet holes and survivorship bias, lottery economics, the Bible-code controversy, smoking and lung cancer) to show how mathematical reasoning corrects misleading intuitions. Recurrent lessons: avoid naive linear extrapolation, beware survivorship bias and selection effects, understand expected value, recognize regression to the mean as an artifact, and treat statistical significance and p-values with caution.

## Key Claims

1. Mathematics is the formalization of common sense, a systematic method for 'not being wrong'.
2. Survivorship bias distorts inference: Wald argued armor belongs where returning planes show no bullet holes; the same logic explains overstated mutual-fund return studies.
3. Linear extrapolation is a common error; relationships that look straight locally are curved globally.
4. Statistical significance is widely misused: 'significant' means detectable, not important, and with enough data almost any effect becomes detectable.
5. The null hypothesis is essentially always false literally, so p-value thresholds flag existence of an effect, not its magnitude.
6. Regression to the mean is a statistical phenomenon often misread as a causal or moral one.

## Questions Raised

- When is a detected effect large enough to matter, as opposed to merely statistically detectable?
- How should significance testing be reformed or supplemented (e.g. with Bayesian reasoning)?
- How do we correct everyday and professional inferences for survivorship and selection effects?
