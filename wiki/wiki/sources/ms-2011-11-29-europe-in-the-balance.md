# Sovereign Subjects: Europe in the Balance

**Authors:** Arnaud Marès · **Year:** 2011 · **Venue:** Morgan Stanley Research · **Type:** report

## Summary

Marès argues that the euro-area crisis is a self-fulfilling 'run' on both banks and governments that began with the Greek default and was intensified by breaking the taboo of euro exit. He frames the December 9 EU summit as Europe's 'Hamiltonian moment', a bifurcation point between debt assumption (fiscal federalism) and a debt jubilee (wide-scale repudiation). The note contends that only a credible lender of last resort, ensuring solvent governments never become illiquid, can stop the run. It maintains this requires both permanent binding fiscal control and a central funding mechanism, not ECB action alone.

## Key Claims

1. The European sovereign crisis is a self-fulfilling 'run' on banks and governments set off by the Greek default without a backstop for solvent sovereigns.
2. Only fiscal federalism combining binding fiscal control with a permanent central funding mechanism can serve as a credible lender of last resort.
3. ECB intervention alone cannot stabilise because, lacking control over national fiscal policy, it would be politically destabilising and fuel break-up fears.
4. Government solvency is inherently subjective absent binding and permanent fiscal constraints.
