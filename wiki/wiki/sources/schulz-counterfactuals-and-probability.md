# Counterfactuals and Probability

**Authors:** Moritz Schulz · **Venue:** Oxford University Press · **Type:** book

## Summary

Moritz Schulz studies the behaviour of counterfactual conditionals under uncertainty, arguing that contexts of uncertainty provide rich evidence for central debates about counterfactuals. Our graded credences in counterfactuals follow a puzzling pattern captured by an Evaluation Constraint linking subjective probability to counterfactual chance (parallel to Adams's thesis for indicatives). Standard Lewis-Stalnaker semantics is too strong and the 'near-miss' proposal too weak; the Evaluation Constraint inherits triviality results. Schulz's positive proposal is a non-standard truth-conditional semantics built on Hilbert's epsilon-operator: a counterfactual is true when its consequent holds at an arbitrarily selected relevant antecedent-world.

## Key Claims

1. Our credences in counterfactuals exhibit a stable pattern (high-but-not-full confidence in lottery cases) that standard semantics cannot accommodate.
2. An Evaluation Constraint governs counterfactuals: subjective probability should equal counterfactual chance, analogous to Adams's thesis.
3. Standard Lewis-Stalnaker truth conditions are too strong while the near-miss proposal is too weak.
4. The Evaluation Constraint inherits triviality results paralleling Lewis's theorems against Adams's thesis.
5. Counterfactual chance can be defined by conditionalizing chance on the selection function's relevant antecedent-worlds (generalized imaging).
6. The epsilon-operator semantics provides truth conditions that survive the puzzle via limited epistemic access.

## Questions Raised

- Do counterfactuals express ordinary propositions with truth conditions, or perform a non-propositional function?
- Does the epsilon-operator semantics introduce an unacceptable indeterminacy in truth conditions?
- Can the view be extended from counterfactuals to indicative conditionals?
