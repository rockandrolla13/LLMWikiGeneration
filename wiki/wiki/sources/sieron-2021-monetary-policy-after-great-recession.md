# Monetary Policy after the Great Recession: The Role of Interest Rates

**Authors:** Arkadiusz Sieroń · **Year:** 2021 · **Venue:** Routledge International Studies in Money and Banking · **Type:** book

## Summary

Arkadiusz Sieroń (University of Wrocław; Austrian-school economist) argues the standard understanding of the monetary transmission mechanism is flawed: conventional theory assumes low rates always stimulate growth via credit, ignoring unintended channels (risk-taking, debt accumulation, zombification) that can render aggressive easing counterproductive. The book proceeds channel by channel (interest-rate, portfolio-balance, risk-taking, resource allocation/zombie firms, the neutral rate, NIRP, the debt trap). The core normative claim, drawing on the Austrian tradition, is that the natural interest rate reflects time preference and performs essential signaling and allocation functions; suppressing market rates distorts the structure of production and sustains nonviable zombie firms.

## Key Claims

1. The standard monetary transmission mechanism is flawed because it assumes low rates always stimulate growth and ignores unintended channels.
2. The weakest US post-war recovery coincided with the most aggressive peacetime monetary and fiscal policy.
3. Ultralow rates encourage a risk-taking channel - a 'search for yield' into riskier assets.
4. Ultralow rates zombify the economy by keeping nonviable firms alive, misallocating resources and depressing productivity.
5. The neutral interest rate is not independent of monetary policy, so there is no genuine 'neutral interest rate policy'.
6. NIRP and the zero lower bound generate distortions rather than reliable stimulus; the interest rate is a crucial signaling and allocation variable, not merely the cost of borrowing.

## Questions Raised

- Why was the post-Great-Recession recovery the weakest post-war US expansion despite aggressive easing?
- Can monetary policy stimulate an economy mired in debt overhang and balance-sheet recession?
- What are the long-run productivity and allocative costs of keeping zombie firms alive?
