# Investing Amid Low Expected Returns: Making the Most When Markets Offer the Least

**Authors:** Antti Ilmanen · **Year:** 2022 · **Venue:** John Wiley & Sons · **Type:** book

## Summary

Ilmanen's 2022 book confronts the secular challenge of low expected returns: after decades of falling discount rates, nearly all major asset classes have become expensive. Framed around the Serenity Prayer, it argues investors face three responses - take more risk, accept lower returns, or build a better-diversified portfolio with less-compressed return sources. It decomposes long-run returns into liquid asset-class premia, illiquidity premia, and four long/short style premia (value, momentum, carry, defensive), presents roughly a century of evidence emphasizing Sharpe ratios, and addresses portfolio construction, costs/fees, ESG, tactical timing, and investor bad habits (notably multiyear return chasing). A successor to his 2011 'Expected Returns'.

## Key Claims

1. Forward-looking expected returns are historically low because falling discount rates produced windfall capital gains; rearview-mirror expectations breed complacency.
2. Investors face three responses: take more risk, accept lower returns, or diversify into less-correlated/less-compressed sources.
3. Roughly a century of evidence supports nine rewarded premia: equity, bond/term, credit, commodity asset-class premia plus value, momentum, carry, and defensive style premia.
4. Sharpe ratio is the preferred scale-invariant metric; long/short style premia scaled to ~5% volatility delivered ~2.5-4.5% average premium each.
5. Valuation changes can distort realized and expected returns even over 70-year horizons, so estimates should not credit a strategy for becoming more expensive.
6. Trading costs and fees, risk management, and portfolio construction matter even more in a low-return environment; multiyear return chasing is a primary investor bad habit.

## Questions Raised

- Are the four umbrella style premia sufficient to capture rewarded alternative risk premia?
- Who takes the other side, and do crowding/capacity concerns threaten persistence?
- How much should investors rely on tactical timing when valuation-based equity timing has performed poorly?
