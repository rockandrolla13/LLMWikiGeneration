# Banking, debt, and currency crises in developed countries: Stylized facts and early warning indicators

**Authors:** Jan Babecky, Tomas Havranek, Jakub Mateju, Marek Rusnak, Katerina Smidkova, Borek Vasicek · **Year:** 2014 · **Venue:** Journal of Financial Stability · **Type:** paper

## Summary

This paper constructs a new quarterly dataset of banking, debt, and currency crisis episodes in 40 developed (EU and OECD) countries over 1970-2010, aggregating ten published crisis databases and validating via a survey of central-bank country experts. Using a panel VAR with recursive identification, it documents that banking and debt crises are interrelated and both typically precede currency crises; banking crises are the most persistent and costly (mean cumulative GDP loss ~6%). On a reduced subsample, Bayesian model averaging over ~30 indicators selects the deviation of domestic private credit to GDP from trend as the single most robust predictor of both banking and currency crises, with money-market rates and the U.S. BAA corporate bond spread as additional banking-crisis leading indicators.

## Key Claims

1. A new quarterly database of banking, debt, and currency crises was assembled for 40 EU/OECD countries (1970-2010), aggregating ten studies and validated by a country-expert survey.
2. Banking and debt crises are interrelated and both typically precede currency crises, while currency crises do not significantly feed back into banking crises.
3. Banking crises are the most persistent (mean duration 8.4 quarters) and costly (mean cumulative GDP loss ~6%, not fully recovered after six years).
4. BMA identifies the deviation of domestic private credit to GDP from trend as the single most robust early warning indicator for both banking and currency crises.
5. Rising money market rates and a decreasing U.S. BAA corporate bond spread are additional leading indicators of banking crises.
6. Crises tend to be preceded by economic booms, with risk build-up highest when market risk perceptions are lowest.

## Questions Raised

- How can deviations of domestic private credit from trend be reliably estimated in real time?
- To what extent do the relationships hold out-of-sample given in-sample variable selection?
- Why do developed economies show no significant feedback from currency to banking crises?
