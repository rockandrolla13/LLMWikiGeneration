# Explanation in Causal Inference: Methods for Mediation and Interaction

**Authors:** Tyler J. VanderWeele · **Year:** 2015 · **Venue:** Oxford University Press · **Type:** book

## Summary

VanderWeele provides the first book-length treatment of mediation and interaction analysis from a counterfactual (potential-outcomes) perspective. The central thesis is that 'explanation' means understanding mechanisms, illuminated through mediation (decomposing an effect into pathways through and around an intermediate variable) and interaction (how the joint effect of two exposures departs from their separate effects). Part One develops mediation (natural direct/indirect effects, controlled direct effects, sensitivity analysis); Part Two develops interaction (additive vs multiplicative scales, sufficient-cause synergism); the book culminates in a four-way decomposition unifying mediation and interaction and extends to spillover effects.

## Key Claims

1. Mediation and interaction are the two principal tools for explaining causal mechanisms, both requiring counterfactual definitions.
2. Natural direct/indirect effects and controlled direct effects give causally meaningful mediation quantities the classic regression approach does not.
3. Identification of natural effects requires four no-unmeasured-confounding assumptions, which are strong and untestable, motivating sensitivity analysis.
4. Statistical interaction is distinct from mechanistic (sufficient-cause) interaction; the scale (additive vs multiplicative) materially changes conclusions.
5. A four-way decomposition partitions a total effect into components due to neither, interaction only, mediation only, and both.
6. Mediation and interaction concepts extend to interference/spillover settings in social networks.

## Questions Raised

- How can mediation methods be made robust when the no-exposure-induced-confounding assumption is violated?
- How can the counterfactual framework be synthesized with less formal social-science mediation methods?
- What are the limits of inferring biological/mechanistic interaction from observational statistical interaction?
