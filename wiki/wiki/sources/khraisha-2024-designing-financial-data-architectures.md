# Designing Financial Data Architectures

**Authors:** Tamer Khraisha  
**Year:** 2024  
**Type:** book  
**Markdown source:** `markdown_output/khraisha-2024-designing-financial-data-architectures.md`

## Summary

An Early Release O'Reilly book (first release 2025-08-06, first edition December 2026) by Tamer Khraisha titled "Designing Financial Data Architectures: Patterns and Principles for AI, Analytics, and Operational Efficiency." Published by O'Reilly Media. The book addresses the challenge of managing the complexity of financial data at scale, arguing that finance has evolved into a full-fledged information industry where data is no longer a byproduct but central to operations, innovation, and competition. Only Chapters 1 and 2 were available in the Early Release read (first 250 lines). Chapter 1 covers finance basics and the financial data ecosystem; subsequent chapters cover data modelling, governance, reference data, operational systems, ledger systems, messaging systems, and analytical systems. The book targets readers from both financial and non-financial backgrounds who work with financial data infrastructure.

## Key Claims

- Finance has evolved into a full-fledged information industry where data is at the core of operations, innovation, and competition, not merely a byproduct.
- Managing financial data complexity requires a disciplined, structured approach — financial data architecture — rather than ad hoc solutions.
- The financial data ecosystem is shaped by a wide range of data-generating mechanisms: trading, payments, FX markets, securities issuance, corporate actions, lending, regulatory filings, macroeconomic policy decisions, investment management, speculation/hedging, insurance, financial messaging, and operational settlements.
- Derivatives are considered the most complex financial instruments due to dependence on underlying assets, complex pricing models, variety of contract structures, and sophisticated uses.
- Securitisation transforms non-tradable instruments (e.g., loans) into tradable securities by pooling and selling shares of that pool to investors.
- Financial messaging standards include SWIFT, FIX, and ISO 20022.
- Regulatory reporting obligations include SEC filings and Basel III compliance.
