# Limited arbitrage between equity and credit markets

**Authors:** Nikunj Kapadia, Xiaoling Pu · **Year:** 2012 · **Venue:** Journal of Financial Economics · **Type:** paper

## Summary

Kapadia and Pu document that short-horizon pricing discrepancies across a firm's equity and credit (CDS) markets are common and that an economically significant proportion are genuine anomalies, indicating a lack of integration. Using daily five-year CDS spreads from Markit matched to CRSP/Compustat for 214 U.S. non-financial firms over 2001-2009, they propose a nonparametric measure of market integration (kappa) based on the concordance of stock-price and CDS-spread changes implied by the Merton model, equal to the Kendall rank correlation. Time variation in integration is related to firm-specific impediments to arbitrage: lower CDS liquidity and higher idiosyncratic risk reduce integration. Simple capital-structure-arbitrage trades earn about 6% per annum over the sample.

## Key Claims

1. Over five-day intervals, 41% of relative stock and CDS-spread movements are Merton-model pricing discrepancies; the fraction declines with horizon, consistent with slow-moving capital.
2. Discrepancies coincide with economically large moves (at 50 days, mean absolute stock return 9.1% and CDS change 39 bps in the wrong direction).
3. An economically significant share of discrepancies is anomalous; capital-structure-arbitrage convergence trades earn ~6% per annum over 2001-2009.
4. Lower CDS market liquidity and higher idiosyncratic risk reduce equity-credit integration; for investment-grade firms higher equity liquidity raises integration.
5. During the 2008-2009 crisis, credit-market illiquidity had a larger negative impact on integration for riskier, more volatile firms.
6. The findings reconcile Schaefer-Strebulaev (2008) with Collin-Dufresne, Goldstein, Martin (2001): mispricing is identifiable but integration depends on arbitrage costs.

## Questions Raised

- What determines cross-sectional and time-series variation in the prevalence of pricing discrepancies?
- What are the relative roles of systematic versus firm-specific impediments to integration?
- Over what horizons must structural credit-risk models be tested to ensure markets are integrated?
