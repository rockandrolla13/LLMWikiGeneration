# Cross-Asset Dispatches: Melting Up Is Hard to Do

**Authors:** Andrew Sheets, Phanikiran L Naraparaju, Wanting Low, Naomi Z Poole, Serena W Tang, Adam Virgadamo · **Year:** 2019 · **Venue:** Morgan Stanley Research · **Type:** report

## Summary

Morgan Stanley examines the possibility of an equity 'melt-up' and concludes it is unlikely in the US. Defining a melt-up as a historically large move from a high starting point, they show such episodes account for just 1-2% of historical observations and, unlike today, typically begin after only modest prior returns, with stronger earnings growth and greater investor fear. They are skeptical of a US melt-up because their economists see no Fed insurance cut and they are below consensus on 2019 earnings, arguing the more likely bull case would originate overseas via better China growth. They note that during past melt-ups credit spreads barely tightened and volatility was largely unchanged, suggesting call options are the best risk-adjusted way to add beta.

## Key Claims

1. Melt-ups account for just 1-2% of historical observations and usually start after only modest prior returns.
2. A US melt-up is unlikely given no forecast Fed insurance cut and below-consensus 2019 earnings.
3. The more likely bull case lies overseas, if better China growth pulls up other regions.
4. During melt-ups credit spreads fell only ~7bp and volatility was largely unchanged while the S&P 500 rose ~13% over three months.

## Questions Raised

- Why do credit and volatility under-price the risk created by a speculative melt-up?
- Could overseas growth, particularly in China, catalyze the bull case instead of the US?
