# Monetary Policy in Times of Crisis: A Tale of Two Decades of the European Central Bank

**Authors:** Massimo Rostagno, Carlo Altavilla, Giacomo Carboni, Wolfgang Lemke, Roberto Motto, Arthur Saint Guilhem, Jonathan Yiangou · **Year:** 2021 · **Venue:** Oxford University Press · **Type:** book

## Summary

Written by seven ECB economists, this volume narrates the first two decades of ECB monetary policy (1999-2018) as a 'tale of two regimes': a Great-Moderation first regime of inflationary supply shocks navigated with a credibility-building framework (a quantitative price-stability definition, a two-pillar strategy, a medium-term orientation), and a post-Lehman second regime of endemic instability, a lower bound, and disinflationary demand shocks. The central claim is that the framework that stabilized inflation in the first regime became a liability in the second: when negative demand shocks dominate at the lower bound, the core-headline inflation correlation flips from negative to positive. The second half traces the ECB's shift from 'separation' to 'combination' and the crisis toolkit (SMP, OMT, LTRO/TLTRO, APP/PSPP, NIRP, forward guidance), supported by DSGE and threshold-BVAR counterfactuals.

## Key Claims

1. The ECB's first two decades split into two regimes: inflationary pro-cyclical supply shocks, and post-crisis endemic instability with disinflationary demand shocks.
2. The 'below close to 2%' definition functioned as a self-stabilizing automatic stabilizer in the first regime, anchoring expectations so the forward curve adjusted with muted policy-rate moves.
3. The same ceiling becomes destabilizing in the second regime: at the lower bound the core-headline inflation correlation flips from negative to positive.
4. The ECB evolved from a 'separation principle' to 'combination' (actively deploying the balance sheet), and from the SMP through OMT, NIRP, forward guidance, TLTRO, and APP/PSPP.
5. OMT required recognition that the crisis had become a loss of faith in the currency union, conviction that the ECB owns euro-area inflation determination, and the creation of the ESM.
6. Counterfactual DSGE/BVAR exercises quantify that non-standard measures materially lowered sovereign yields and supported GDP and inflation.

## Questions Raised

- Was the asymmetry of the 'below close to 2%' framework a design flaw contributing to the 2010s disinflation?
- Did the euro area definitively enter the destabilizing second regime?
- Was the 2011 rate hike a policy error attributable to the price-stability-ceiling logic?
