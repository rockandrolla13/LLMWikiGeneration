# Fed Up! Success, Excess and Crisis Through the Eyes of a Hedge Fund Macro Trader

**Authors:** Colin Lancaster · **Year:** 2021 · **Type:** book

## Summary

A first-person narrative by global macro hedge fund trader Colin Lancaster covering October 2019 through June 2020, structured in three parts ('The Late Stages of a Bubble', 'The Crash', 'The Aftermath'). It chronicles late-cycle excess, the COVID-19 market crash of March 2020 (compounded by the Saudi-Russia oil price war), and the rapid recovery driven by unprecedented Federal Reserve and fiscal intervention, interleaving real-time trading decisions with personal and moral commentary. The central thesis is that decades of accommodative monetary policy inflated an epic debt bubble, and that the 2020 crisis exposed the system's reliance on the Fed as backstop. It frames discretionary global macro as a strategy that thrives when the world is falling apart.

## Key Claims

1. Decades of easy monetary policy built a debt bubble; US corporate credit grew from ~$2T in 2008 to ~$7T by 2020, heavily weighted toward BBB and single-A paper.
2. The Fed's late-2019 repo injections and T-bill purchases ('not QE' QE) plus rate cuts pivoted markets higher and reinforced reliance on central-bank support.
3. The March 2020 crash was a compound shock - COVID-19 plus the Saudi-Russia oil price war - driving correlations toward one and triggering equity circuit breakers.
4. Discretionary global macro outperforms in crises because its broad cross-asset mandate lets managers profit from dislocations.
5. Quant and factor strategies plus QE have structurally changed markets; algorithmic momentum amplified the 2020 selloff.
6. Policy response was extraordinary: the Fed cut to zero and restarted aggressive QE, and governments rolled out large fiscal packages.

## Questions Raised

- Can the Fed indefinitely backstop asset markets, or does each intervention deepen the underlying fragility?
- Does the dominance of quant strategies and algorithmic trading make crashes more violent?
- Will post-2020 stimulus reignite inflation or merely re-inflate the bubble?
