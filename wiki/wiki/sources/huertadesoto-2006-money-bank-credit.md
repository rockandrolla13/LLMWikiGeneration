# Money, Bank Credit, and Economic Cycles

**Authors:** Jesús Huerta de Soto · **Year:** 2006 · **Venue:** Ludwig von Mises Institute · **Type:** book

## Summary

A comprehensive Austrian-school treatise arguing that fractional-reserve banking is the root cause of recurring economic cycles. It builds the case in three layers: a legal-historical layer showing the monetary irregular-deposit contract demands a 100-percent reserve and that fractional reserves constitute a misappropriation; an economic-theory layer applying Austrian capital theory and the Mises-Hayek circulation-credit theory of the business cycle; and a policy layer proposing a 100-percent reserve banking reform with free choice in currency. The central thesis is that credit expansion unbacked by prior voluntary saving artificially lowers the interest rate, distorts the structure of production toward more capital-intensive stages, and seeds malinvestment that the market must eventually liquidate in a crisis.

## Key Claims

1. Fractional-reserve banking violates the essential legal nature of the monetary irregular-deposit contract, which requires a 100-percent reserve.
2. Banks lending out demand deposits create money (fiduciary media) ex nihilo, expanding credit beyond voluntary saving.
3. Credit expansion unbacked by real saving artificially lowers interest rates and distorts the structure of production toward higher-order stages.
4. The resulting boom is self-destructive: the Ricardo effect, forced saving, and rising rates expose malinvestments, triggering a crisis.
5. The Austrian (circulation-credit) theory of the business cycle, not Monetarist or Keynesian aggregate models, correctly explains recurring cycles.
6. A 100-percent reserve requirement plus free banking and free choice in currency would eliminate the credit-driven business cycle.

## Questions Raised

- Is a 100-percent reserve requirement enforceable without a central authority that itself distorts the system?
- How would a transition to 100-percent reserves be sequenced without triggering deflation/crisis?
- Does empirical evidence discriminate the Austrian theory from Monetarist and Keynesian alternatives?
