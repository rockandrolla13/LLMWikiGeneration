# Bayes and Base Rates: How History Can Guide Our Assessment of the Future

**Authors:** Michael J. Mauboussin, Dan Callahan · **Year:** 2026 · **Venue:** Counterpoint Global Insights (Morgan Stanley Investment Management) · **Type:** report

## Summary

A Counterpoint Global Insights (Morgan Stanley) research note (10 February 2026) arguing that investors should assess aggressive AI-era growth forecasts using a Bayesian mindset anchored on empirical base rates. Bayes' Theorem combines a prior with recent data; the practical value lies in continually updating in proportion to the weight of evidence (per Tetlock and the Good Judgment Project). The note applies this to OpenAI's $145bn 2029 revenue projection (108% five-year CAGR, never achieved historically for firms of that size - a ~9.5 standard-deviation outcome) and Oracle's cloud forecast (~75% CAGR). It draws on Flyvbjerg's database of 16,000 large projects (reference-class forecasting) and Porter's preemptive-strategy framework to interpret AI capacity announcements.

## Key Claims

1. OpenAI's forecast of $145B 2029 revenue from $3.7B in 2024 implies a 108% five-year CAGR, an outcome no US public company of that starting size achieved in 1950-2024 - roughly a 9.5 standard-deviation event.
2. Oracle's cloud revenue forecast of $166B by fiscal 2030 implies a ~75% five-year CAGR, also unprecedented.
3. Bayes' Theorem cannot operate on a zero prior, so heuristics such as 3/N and Laplace smoothing yield priors below one-tenth of one percent.
4. Superforecasters succeed less by formal Bayes' Theorem than by gradual updating in proportion to evidence.
5. Flyvbjerg's database of 16,000 large projects shows fewer than 9% finish on budget and on time, motivating reference-class forecasting.
6. Growth does not create value unless return on investment exceeds the cost of capital; OpenAI reported roughly -$9B free cash flow in 2025.

## Questions Raised

- Will rapid ChatGPT adoption translate into paying customers and revenue?
- Can base rates legitimately be revised upward given technology-diffusion speed?
- Will the AI infrastructure boom repeat the late-1990s telecom overcapacity pattern?
