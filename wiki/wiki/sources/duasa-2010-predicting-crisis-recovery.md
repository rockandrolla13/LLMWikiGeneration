# Predicting the Global Crisis Recovery Period: Lessons from the 1997 Crisis

**Authors:** Jarita Duasa, Nursilah Ahmad · **Year:** 2010 · **Venue:** Global Economic Review, Vol. 39, No. 1 · **Type:** paper

## Summary

Duasa and Ahmad identify the best leading indicator for forecasting Malaysia's recovery from the 2008 global financial crisis, drawing lessons from the 1997 Asian crisis. They build two-variable vector error correction models pairing economic-performance proxies (industrial production, GDP, share prices, overnight rate) against three composite cyclical indices, using monthly/quarterly data 1980-2000. They find no cointegration between macroeconomic performance and indicators but a cointegrating relation between the lagging index and financial performance; the lagging index is the best financial-performance indicator, and half-life estimates imply roughly two to four years of recovery.

## Key Claims

1. No cointegration is found between macroeconomic performance (IPI, GDP) and any indicator, so they cannot reliably forecast real economic recovery.
2. The trace test finds cointegrating vectors pairing the lagging index with share prices and with the overnight rate.
3. The lagging index is the single best indicator of financial performance for Malaysia in both crisis contexts.
4. Half-life estimates imply Malaysia recovered from the 1997 crisis in roughly two to four years, projecting a similar window for 2008.
5. Granger causality tests show indicators and financial performance do not Granger-cause each other.

## Questions Raised

- What indicator can reliably forecast the recovery of the real (macro) economy?
- How would adding demand and supply shock controls change the recovery estimates?
- Is assuming 2008 and 1997 environments are similar sufficient to transfer half-life estimates?
