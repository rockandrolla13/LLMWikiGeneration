# Recursive Macroeconomic Theory

**Authors:** Lars Ljungqvist, Thomas J. Sargent · **Year:** 2012 · **Venue:** The MIT Press (3rd edition) · **Type:** book

## Summary

A canonical graduate textbook arguing that recursive methods are a pervasive, unifying technology for modern macroeconomics. The organizing thesis - 'the imperialism of recursive methods' - is that a vast range of dynamic problems can be formulated via a state vector, a transition law, and a policy function, with optimization reduced to a tradeoff between current utility and a continuation value (dynamic programming). The book is structured around recurring ideas: complete-markets competitive equilibrium, incomplete-markets heterogeneous-agent (Bewley) models, models of fiat money, policy-irrelevance results, Ramsey optimal taxation, recursive contracts ('dynamic programming squared'), credible government policy, and search-and-matching theory of unemployment. The toolkit leans on Markov chains, state-space representations, the Kalman filter, VARs, and the optimal linear regulator.

## Key Claims

1. Recursive methods - a state vector, transition law, and policy function - provide a unifying technology across asset pricing, growth, public finance, monetary economics, contracts, and search.
2. Dynamic optimization reduces to a per-period tradeoff between current utility and a continuation value via Bellman equations; identifying the state variables is the central step.
3. Time-0 Arrow-Debreu trading and sequential trading in one-period Arrow securities deliver equivalent allocations in complete markets.
4. History-dependent allocations in recursive contracts can be made recursive by promoting promised continuation utility to a state variable.
5. The Ramsey optimal-taxation problem is solved by a primal approach; under standard assumptions the optimal long-run capital tax is zero.
6. Reputation can substitute for commitment: multiple self-confirming expectation systems support sustainable government policies as subgame-perfect equilibria.

## Questions Raised

- Under what market-structure and preference modifications can asset-pricing models match Hansen-Jagannathan bounds and resolve the equity premium puzzle?
- When do Ricardian-equivalence and Modigliani-Miller policy-irrelevance results fail?
- How should optimal taxation and debt be designed when state-contingent government debt is unavailable?
