# The Level and Persistence of Growth Rates

**Authors:** Louis K. C. Chan, Jason Karceski, Josef Lakonishok · **Year:** 2001 · **Venue:** NBER Working Paper No. 8282 · **Type:** paper

## Summary

NBER Working Paper w8282 examines the historical distribution and persistence of long-term growth rates of corporate operating performance across U.S. stocks (Compustat, 1951-1998), using net sales, operating income, and income before extraordinary items. The motivating puzzle is that at year-end 1999 a large fraction of market cap traded at very high P/E multiples requiring sustained growth (~29%/year for ten years) that is historically extremely rare. The core empirical contribution is a nonparametric 'runs' test for persistence: while sales growth shows persistence, there is essentially no persistence in bottom-line earnings growth - multi-year above-median runs occur about as often as chance. IBES analyst long-term growth forecasts are systematically over-optimistic and add little predictive power beyond one year.

## Key Claims

1. Median long-term operating-performance growth matches real GDP growth (~3-3.5% real) over 1950-98.
2. Spectacular growth is rare: only about 10% of firms exceed 18% annual growth over ten years.
3. Sales growth exhibits persistence, but earnings growth shows essentially no persistence - multi-year above-median runs occur about as often as chance.
4. Lack of earnings persistence holds even for technology, pharmaceutical, glamour, and past high-growth subsets.
5. IBES analyst long-term growth forecasts are systematically over-optimistic, most so for high-forecast glamour stocks.
6. Beyond one year, analyst forecasts add little predictive power over a naive dividend-yield model; competitive forces revert profitability toward a normal rate.

## Questions Raised

- If earnings growth is essentially unpredictable beyond a year, what justifies the large dispersion in P/E multiples and analyst forecasts?
- Are persistent analyst over-optimism and dispersion driven by cognitive biases or brokerage incentives?
- How much does survivorship bias inflate measured persistence, especially for volatile glamour stocks?
