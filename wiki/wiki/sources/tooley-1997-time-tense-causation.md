# Time, Tense, and Causation

**Authors:** Michael Tooley · **Year:** 1997 · **Venue:** Clarendon Press / Oxford University Press · **Type:** book

## Summary

Michael Tooley defends an 'intermediate' tensed metaphysics of time on which the past and present are real but the future is not, sitting between standard tensed (A-theory) and tenseless (B-theory) positions. The load-bearing argument is the 'argument from causation': causation presupposes a dynamic world in which the future is unreal. Tooley grounds the time-causation link in a theory of causation that is realist (anti-reductionist), singularist (causal relations do not presuppose causal laws), and probabilistic. He also defends a temporally relativized notion of truth ('truth at a time') and reconciles absolute simultaneity with Special Relativity via a modified theory.

## Key Claims

1. The past and present are real but the future is not; the world is dynamic ('growing').
2. Tooley's view is intermediate: tensed facts are logically supervenient on which tenseless facts are actual as of a time.
3. The 'argument from causation' is fundamental: causation presupposes a dynamic world in which the future is unreal.
4. The correct theory of causation is realist (anti-reductionist), singularist (causal relations do not presuppose causal laws), and connected to probabilities.
5. Backward causation cannot be shown logically impossible by standard arguments, which only rule out causal loops.
6. A temporally relativized notion of truth ('truth at a time') is required for tensed statements, justified by a correspondence theory.

## Questions Raised

- Can a dynamic world with tensed-facts-supervening-on-tenseless-facts be shown coherent and preferable to both standard views?
- Can backward causation be proven logically impossible without merely ruling out causal loops?
- Does a singularist, realist, probabilistic theory of causation entail a dynamic world and the unreality of the future?
