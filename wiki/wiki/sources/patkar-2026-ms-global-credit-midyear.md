# Morgan Stanley Global Credit Webcast - May 2026: Debating the Mid-Year Outlook

**Authors:** Vishwas Patkar, Vishy Tirupattur, David Hamburger, Aron Becker, Kelvin Pang · **Year:** 2026 · **Venue:** Morgan Stanley Research (Global Credit Webcast) · **Type:** report

## Summary

A Morgan Stanley Research sell-side webcast note (26 May 2026) presenting the firm's mid-year 2026 outlook across global credit. The US team frames a capex-driven cycle: accelerating AI/hyperscaler capex (forecast >$1.1tn in 2027) underpins a robust macro backdrop and strong earnings, with credit lagging equities and modestly wider IG spreads alongside record IG/HY supply. Private credit is a central theme - direct-lending defaults forecast to rise to 8% (software-concentrated) but skewed to distressed exchanges, with 'Private Credit 2.0' expanding into infrastructure and asset-based finance. European credit is 'glass half full' (~80bp IG), while Asia credit is an 'uneasy equilibrium' expected to widen. A Merton-model framing links elevated Asian equity valuations to lower credit risk; oil-price (Strait of Hormuz) risk is a common driver.

## Key Claims

1. AI/hyperscaler capex is the dominant macro driver, forecast to exceed $1.1tn in 2027, underpinning a robust US backdrop.
2. Morgan Stanley expects 23% S&P 500 EPS growth in 2026 and views the setup as comparable to 1997/98 and 2005/06, with credit lagging equities.
3. US IG supply is forecast at a record $2.25tn and HY at $440bn on AI financing; HY outperforms, total returns 6%+.
4. Direct-lending / private-credit default rates are forecast to rise to 8%, concentrated in software, but skewed to distressed exchanges with higher recoveries.
5. Private credit risks are judged significant but not systemic given healthy balance sheets and lower fund/BDC leverage than the GFC.
6. 'Private Credit 2.0' extends beyond direct lending into infrastructure PC, real estate debt, and asset-based finance, financing AI infrastructure.
7. Asia IG spreads reflect an uneasy equilibrium; base case to widen to 65bp, bear 130bp under an oil-led recession.

## Questions Raised

- Will the AI-capex cycle sustain, or concentrate credit risk in data-center debt and software direct lending?
- Is the 8% private-credit default forecast contained to software, with the claimed higher recoveries?
- How systemic is private credit given limited bank disclosure on NDFI sub-components?
- Does current pricing leave credit exposed to a sharp repricing if oil escalates?
