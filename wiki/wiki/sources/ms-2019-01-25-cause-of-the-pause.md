# Corporate Credit Research: The Cause of the Pause

**Authors:** Adam S. Richmond, Vishwas Patkar, Frederick T. Fuchs, Felician G. Stratmann, Jingli Li · **Year:** 2019 · **Venue:** Morgan Stanley Research · **Type:** report

## Summary

Morgan Stanley's corporate credit team argues that the dovish shift in Fed rhetoric helped fuel the early-2019 bounce in credit, but the reason the Fed stops hiking matters more than the pause itself. Historically, the bulk of cyclical spread widening occurs from the last rate hike until deep into a rate-cutting cycle, when growth expectations roll over and recession risk rises fastest. They note this cycle is unusual because the Fed is also shrinking its balance sheet, so the end of quantitative tightening could be more of a tailwind for credit than the end of hikes. They maintain a cautious long-term view, believing credit is in a bear market and the easy money in the rally has been made.

## Key Claims

1. Most cyclical credit spread widening historically occurs from the last Fed rate hike through a rate-cutting cycle, when growth slows and recession risk rises.
2. The end of quantitative tightening could be a larger tailwind for credit than the end of rate hikes, potentially pulling forward spread widening.
3. Morgan Stanley remains long-term cautious on US credit, viewing it as in a bear market despite the early-2019 tactical rally.

## Questions Raised

- Will the extent of the 2019 growth and earnings slowdown be interpreted by markets as the end of the cycle?
