# Causality and Explanation

**Authors:** Wesley C. Salmon · **Year:** 1998 · **Venue:** Oxford University Press · **Type:** book

## Summary

A collection of 26 essays by Wesley C. Salmon gathering three decades of his work on causality and scientific explanation. The point of departure is Hume's critique that the connection between cause and effect cannot be derived from formal reasoning. Salmon argues twentieth-century science opens a path to a solution: causal connections can be identified with physical processes that transmit causal influence ('at-at' theory), explanation comes in compatible causal/mechanical and unificationist forms, and causality has essential probabilistic features so a theory of explanation must allow for indeterministic contexts.

## Key Claims

1. Hume was correct that an effect cannot be logically deduced from its cause; any cause-effect connection is not one of logical necessity.
2. Causal connections can be identified with physical processes that transmit causal influence, formalized via the 'at-at' theory.
3. Scientific explanation comes in two compatible types: causal/mechanical explanation and explanation by theoretical unification.
4. Causality has essential probabilistic features, so explanation must allow for indeterministic contexts including quantum mechanics.
5. The view that explanations are simply arguments is mistaken; explanatory asymmetry must be defended.
6. Functional and teleological explanations can be reconstructed as legitimate causal explanations via consequence-etiology.

## Questions Raised

- Can the cause-effect connection be grounded in physical processes rather than logical necessity?
- Does causality operate in indeterministic contexts such as quantum mechanics, and what form must explanation take there?
- Can causal and unificationist accounts of explanation be reconciled rather than treated as rivals?
