# The Determinants of Credit Spread Changes

**Authors:** Pierre Collin-Dufresne, Robert S. Goldstein, J. Spencer Martin · **Year:** 2001 · **Venue:** The Journal of Finance, Vol. 56, No. 6 (2001); SSRN working paper, 1999 · **Type:** paper

## Summary

Collin-Dufresne, Goldstein, and Martin investigate which variables explain monthly changes in corporate credit spreads, using quotes on non-callable, non-puttable industrial bonds from the Lehman/Warga Fixed Income Database over July 1988 to December 1997. They construct proxies for the determinants suggested by structural (contingent-claims) models of default: changes in firm leverage (or equity return), the 10-year Treasury rate level and its square, yield-curve slope (10-year minus 2-year), volatility (VIX), the probability/magnitude of a downward jump (slope of the implied-volatility smirk on S&P 500 futures options), and the business climate (S&P 500 return). A first-pass regression on each bond yields adjusted R-squared values of only roughly 19-25%, so the theoretically motivated variables have surprisingly limited explanatory power. Principal components analysis of the residuals shows a single common factor explains about 71% of the residual variation, suggesting the corporate bond market is driven by an aggregate, possibly liquidity-related, supply/demand factor independent of equity and Treasury markets.

## Key Claims

1. Variables that theory predicts should drive credit spread changes (leverage, interest rates, volatility, jump risk, business climate) explain only about 25% of the variation in individual-bond credit spread changes (adjusted R-squared 19-25%).
2. The residuals from the first-pass regression are highly cross-correlated; principal components analysis shows a single common factor explains about 71% of the residual variation.
3. An expanded 'kitchen sink' regression raises adjusted R-squared only to about 31%, and the first principal component of those residuals still explains about 63% of the remaining variation.
4. An increase in the risk-free rate lowers credit spreads for all bonds, with interest-rate sensitivity increasing across leverage and rating groups.
5. The S&P 500 return is highly significant with a negative sign and is more significant than firm-specific equity returns.
6. Credit spreads respond asymmetrically to volatility: increases in implied volatility sharply widen spreads while decreases have little effect.
7. Aggregate factors dominate firm-specific factors, contradicting the prediction of structural models; results suggest a segmented bond market driven by a bond-specific (possibly liquidity-driven) factor.

## Questions Raised

- What is the economic identity of the single common systematic factor driving credit spread changes that structural models cannot capture?
- Could a better-measured aggregate bond-market liquidity factor explain the common residual factor?
- If stock and bond markets are segmented, why are they segmented?
