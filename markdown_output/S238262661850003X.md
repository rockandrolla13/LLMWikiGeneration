Derivative hedging under transaction costs has attracted considerable attention over the past three decades. Yet comparatively little e®ort has been made towards integrating this problem in the context of trading through a limit order book. In this paper, we propose a simple model for a wealth-optimizing option seller, who hedges his position using a combination of limit and market orders, while facing certain constraints as to how far he can deviate from a targeted (Bachelierian) delta strategy. ## 1. The Problem of Hedging with Transaction Costs 

## 1.1. Introduction and literature survey 

One of the core principles of prudent derivatives trading is the ability to place timely and accurate hedges of option portfolios. Reasons therefore extend beyond the curtailment of risk: by removing the delta-exposure of an option trade, a bet on implied versus realized volatility can be constructed, which in turn can be used to harvest volatility arbitrage (Ahmad and Wilmott, 2005; Ellersgaard et al., 2017). Yet, while hedging remains thoroughly understood in the axiomatic framework of Black–Scholes (1973), the shear empirical implausibility of this ¯nancial landscape in itself calls for an afterthought. Given that markets manifestly are not frictionless, any attempt at replicating a Black–Scholes hedge will theoretically result in in¯nite transaction costs (Zakamouline, 2006). It is precisely this uncomfortable fact which lies at the heart of much of the hedging literature produced over the past three decades. For example, Leland (1985), Hoggard et al. (1994) notably argue that for ¯xed-interval rebalancing of an underlying which incurs transaction costs in proportion to its price,[1] the Black–Scholes equation takes on a modi¯ed volatility in order to match expected costs between derivative and replicating portfolio. While illuminating, this model nonetheless faces criticism from several directions. First, as shown by Kabanov and Safarian (1997), Leland's conjecture of a vanishing hedging error is erroneous; secondly, as pointed out by Hodges et al. (Clewlow and Hodges, 1997; Hodges and Neuberger, 1989), Leland's strategy clearly falls short of attaining any sense of optimality. In its stead, Hodges et al. ingeniously propose a utility indi®erence argument, in which the price of an option is determined as the amount of money required by a rational agent to make him indi®erent to the prospect of being able to sell that option. While this approach uniquely ¯xes the price and optimal hedging strategy in a market with friction, it comes at the cost of diluting the principle of pure no-arbitrage pricing by the subjective utility preferences of the agent. Furthermore, as the Hamilton–Jacobi–Bellman (HJB) equation of the problem in itself is not analytically tractable, one must invariably resort

In this paper, we aim to further bridge the gap between delta hedging and the order book literature. We do so from the perspective of a rational option seller, who derives linear utility from the ¯nancial wealth generated from his dealings, and quadratic utility penalization by deviating from some exogenously speci¯ed hedge target. Speci¯cally, our concern here is the optimal policy the hedger should follow, insofar as the underlying asset can be traded using a combination of limit and market orders. The intuition here is that a hedger who is in close proximity to his delta target will be contended with placing slow and cost e®ective limit orders, while being signi¯cantly o®-target will induce panic buying or selling through costly but instantaneous market orders. For us, the �-strategy we are tracking is for all practical purposes completely generic, although we for illustrative reasons shall restrict our attention to a Bachelierian hedge ratio (Bachelier, 1900). Our framework thus ¯nds its intellectual roots in the limit-market order duality developed by Guilbaud and Pham (mentioned above), and Cartea and Jaimungal (2014), who consider an analogous set-up, albeit from the perspective of a trader who attempts to track the optimal execution algorithm developed by Almgren and Chriss (2000/2001). However, while the Almgren Chriss strategy in itself codi¯es market-microstructure e®ects by virtue of being sensitive to market impact, we stress that the same thing cannot be said of our targeted Bachelier hedge. In this sense, our philosophy sides with the work of Li and Almgren, again mentioned above. 

More interesting variations of the targeted � are conceivable: for example, it would be opportune to explore tracking the Whalley Wilmott boundary, thereby facilitating entire regions of time-price-inventory space in which the hedger posts no orders at all. ## 1.2. Overview 

The structure of the rest of this paper is as follows: in Sec. 2, we explicate the fundamental assumptions of the market and the portfolio manager and state the associated control problem. Section 3 is a survey of the exogenously speci¯ed hedge ratio: a careful analysis shows that the dynamics of the order book is weakly convergent towards the dynamics associated with the targeted delta. Section 4 exposes the Hamilton–Jacobi–Bellman quasi-variational inequality (HJB QVI) associated with the control problem, and suggests a dimensional reduction. Finding ourselves unable to extract an analytic solution thereto, Sec. 5 sets us up for a numerical scheme which is shown to have the desired properties of monotonicity, stability, and consistency as dictated by Barles (2012). Finally, Sec. 6 provides concrete numerical results, comparing the performance of our algorithm with a more naïve approach to �-hedging. Section 7 concludes the paper. 

Remark 2. We formulate our problem through the employ of Poisson point processes (see, e.g., Last and Brandt (1995) for a survey thereof). These constructs ¯nd their natural habitat through the inherent discreteness of the order book, and are instances of Hawkes processes, which have successfully been applied in the high-frequency domain of ¯nancial modeling in recent years, see, e.g., Bacry et al. ## 7. Conclusion 

The emergence of certain stylized properties of the otherwise hyper-complex limit order book beckons us to attempt mathematical modeling thereof. In this paper, we proposed a simple jump model of the mid-price, alongside a probability of having a limit order met which is an exponentially decaying function of the distance to the mid-price. It was shown that the jump model converges weakly to Bachelier's arithmetic Brownian motion for large values of the intensity. Furthermore, under the assumption that a portfolio manager wishing to �-hedge his short call position derives linear utility from his terminal wealth, but incurs quadratic lifetime penalization from deviating from a targeted hedge strategy, we derived numerical values for his optimal limit order quotes and stopping times at which he should switch to pure market orders. Following the standard literature on convergence in the viscosity sense, we proved that our scheme is monotone, stable, and consistent. We 

1850003-24

also exempli¯ed the utility of our algorithm by comparing it to a naïve strategy which deploys nothing but market orders: a clear augmentation of the mean return and a clear reduction in the associated variance were established here. Finally, given the generic nature of our model, we reiterate that it is adaptable to more sophisticated expressions for the targeted hedge ratio in the limit order book.
