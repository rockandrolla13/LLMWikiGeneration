This analysis investigates how liquidity is a®ected by periods of high trade intensity. Using an orderbook constructed directly from CME FIX/FAST messages and timestamped to the millisecond, we test whether the number of changes in the orderbook, the size of the bid–ask spread, and the number of trades in the few seconds before a trade have an e®ect on the book's liquidity in the milliseconds after the trade. Since we calculate liquidity over a period of 100 ms after a trade, we focus on liquidity provided by high-frequency traders (HFTs). We ¯nd evidence consistent – with larger bid ask spreads leading to greater amounts of liquidity being provided by HFT post-trade, and HFT providing liquidity when there is more activity in the orderbook. We further ¯nd that more trades lead to reduced liquidity, consistent with trades incorporating private information, and market makers' fear of being adversely selected when providing liquidity. 

Keywords: High-frequency trading; market making; natural gas markets; futures markets; liquidity provision; price impact; price discovery.

liquidity or by requiring greater compensation to o®set risk/costs of adverse selection (Du±e, 2010; Glosten and Milgrom, 1985; Kyle, 1985; Menkveld and Zoican, 2017). Traders possessing superior ability in acquiring and incorporating information are able to reduce adverse selection risk, subsequently requiring lower compensation when supplying liquidity (Brogaard et al., 2014; Hasbrouck and Saar, 2013). On the other hand, adroit traders may impose adverse selection costs on those unable to respond as quickly, as well as bene¯t by trading on positive information before others are able to respond (DellaVigna and Pollet, 2009; Glosten and Milgrom, 1985; Jovanovic and Menkveld, 2016). The threat of adroit traders imposes monitoring costs on other traders with uncertain information (Du±e, 2010; Stoll, 2000). 

Kyle (1985), Gervais (1997), and later on Hong and Rady (2002) developed models of private information, where uncertain traders learn or rather evolve their information set from the past price and volume. Presently, there is very little research on how market maker's reaction to trades can be conditional on what has occurred in the book prior to the trade. Akin to Hong and Rady (2002) and Biais et al. (1995) we investigate change in liquidity o®ering as conditional on past market outcomes. We employ a unique millisecond timestamped dataset to investigate the e®ects of trade intensity, trade direction, and the bid–ask spread on market liquidity. High-frequency traders (HFTs) are identi¯ed in our analysis by the speed of their reaction to the trade. Our post-trade liquidity metrics measure changes in liquidity from 1 ms to 100 ms after the trade, so we de¯ne HFT as any market participant who can react to a trade within that – latency. We ¯nd evidence consistent with larger bid ask spreads leading to greater amounts of liquidity being provided by HFT post-trade, and HFT providing liquidity when there is more activity in the orderbook. We also ¯nd that increasing the number of trades can reduce the amount of liquidity provided by HFT. 

The remainder of the paper is organized as follows. In the following section we review the related literature. In Sec. 3 we describe the data employed, hypotheses, and methods employed. Section 4 describes the results and Sec. 5 concludes the paper.

The threat of being adversely selected combined with predatory suppliers of liquidity causes liquidity providers to continuously monitor markets for new information and signal changes. This continuous monitoring and adjustment requires automation if traders are to react within milliseconds. To react with such speed, highfrequency traders employ algorithms to manage trades as well as inventory (sometimes referred to as \silicon traders" as opposed to \human traders"). Harris and Hasbrouck (1996) also observe that orders are conditional on the spread direction. 

Groß-Klußmann and Hautsch (2011) examine the continuous and nonscheduled news °ow to intraday market activity to explore high-frequency movements in returns, volatility, and liquidity in response to signals from an automated news engine. They observe that information impact is observed in bid–ask spread, trading volume, and market depth. This is to say that information is conveyed or rather HFT responds to information by changes in spread, volume, and depth. That is, HFT monitors spread, volume, and depth as a source of information in itself, though Hagstr€omer and Norden (2013) report that HFT employs varying strategies and as such, may have varying in°uence on the market.

## 3.1. Data 

Data are Market Depth Data[1] for natural gas futures (ticker: NG) purchased directly from the Chicago Mercantile Exchange (CME). Market Depth Data contains all market messages to and from the CME, and is timestamped to the millisecond. Using this data we can recreate the NG orderbook up to 10 levels deep. The dataset contains all trades and limit order updates, as well as other related information such as the aggressor side of each trade. 

The data are encoded in the CME's FIX/FAST message speci¯cation.[2] We translate the encoded data and extract trade and quote data. With the trade and quote data we build the NG orderbook. The translation scripts used in this analysis are available at: https://github.com/Matt-Brigida/CME-FIXFAST-Translator. 

We choose to use the NYMEX natural gas book in this analysis for a couple of reasons. First, unlike stock, all trades and quotes take place in this one central book. Therefore we have a complete picture of the reaction of the orderbook to trades. Further, since all trading takes place on the same computer server, there is no delay in orders due to location, as there would be if you consolidated all trading in one stock across multiple exchanges into one book. Lastly, NYMEX natural gas is heavily traded and very volatile, which make it more likely that orderbook reactions will be clearly discernable. 

We analyze the data from 16 September 2013 to 27 September 2013 (both dates inclusive). The front month NG contract was for October (ticker: NGV3). 

The following summarizes how we prepared our data on 16 September 2013 for analysis, which is indicative of all days analyzed. There were 54,036 trades in total. Note that our data consists of every entry into the orderbook timestamped to the millisecond, so there can be many entries for a given millisecond. This reduced the number of trades in our sample to 3,828.

## 3.1.1. HFT identi¯cation 

Our method of HFT identi¯cation is a modi¯cation of the method used in Hasbrouck and Saar (2013). They create a measure of low-latency trading activity by focusing on activity within a set time after an event. The event they use is an order cancellation. Speci¯cally, they assume that an order cancellation which is followed by an order submission within 100 ms of the cancellation, is the result of low-latency activity. Similarly, they also classify that an order cancellation followed by an order execution for the same trade direction and quantity as the cancellation, is a low-latency activity. 

In our analysis we use activity in the 100 ms after a trade, instead of after an order cancellation. Speci¯cally, we test for the change in liquidity available in the orderbook after a trade has been executed. That we focus on liquidity provision in this interval restricts our analysis to the subset of liquidity providing HFT. Hasbrouck and Saar (2013) were able to test this method of HFT identi¯cation because their dataset overlapped with a dataset available from the NASDAQ which identi¯ed whether a trade was from a low-latency actor. They found their measure had an over 0.8 correlation with NASDAQ's identi¯cation. 

Lastly, this methodology is unlikely to be a®ected by orders submitted by higher-latency traders who may submit orders in our identi¯cation interval that are unrelated to that trade. There are far more changes to the orderbook

## 4.3. The bid–ask spread 

We ¯nd evidence that larger bid–ask spreads lead to greater amounts of liquidity being provided by HFT post-trade. This is consistent with Hendershott and Riordan (2013) who found algorithmic traders react to new events faster when liquidity is low. 

Speci¯cally, on seven out of 10 days there was a positive relationship – between the pre-trade bid ask spread and the change in post-trade liquidity. Of the remaining days one showed no relationship, one showed a negative relationship, and on one day the evidence was mixed. The day with a negative relationship showed liquidity being reduced on the opposite side of the orderbook from where the aggressor took liquidity ��� e.g., on the bid side if the trade was initiated by a buyer.

The number of changes in the orderbook and the bid–ask spread both increase the amount of liquidity in the orderbook, thereby increasing market depth. However the reduction of liquidity by HFT in reaction to multiple trades in the pre-trade interval is evidence of the detrimental e®ect of HFT. This result is consistent with concerns that, since HFT lacks the af¯rmative requirement to provide liquidity like a traditional market maker, they will cause market disruption by removing liquidity when it is convenient. These results, however, summarize the e®ect of HFT in aggregate, and may not be indicative of the e®ect of individual HFT ¯rms. 

The above results were obtained despite our careful approach of selecting trades of only one contract, and ¯ltering our data by trade type. This reduces our sample of signed trades to less than 1,000 per day, and unsigned (NA) trades to generally less than 5,000 per day. For example, we only have 197 and 243 buy and sell trades, respectively, on 16 September 2013. This small sample increases the t-statistics and p-values. Conversely, since there are 2,277 \Unknown" trades, we are somewhat more likely to ¯nd signi¯cant results based on the sample size. 

## 5. Conclusion 

In this analysis, we have constructed the natural gas futures orderbook (NGV3) with millisecond frequency directly from CME FIX/FAST messages. Then, over a 10-day period, we isolate each trade which is for a single contract. For this set of trades we use three separate measures of trade intensity prior to the trade, and also calculate the change in liquidity on the orderbook post-trade. We then estimate the relationship between the pre-trade measures and post-trade liquidity. We speci¯cally focus on liquidity provision by HFT by calculating the change in liquidity in the 100 ms post-trade. 

Doing so we ¯nd post-trade liquidity is increasing in (1) the number of changes to the orderbook (cancellations and additions) and (2) the size of the bid–ask spread, in the pre-trade interval. In 70% of the days in our sample post-trade liquidity was increasing in both the number of changes in the orderbook and the size of the bid–ask spread, prior to the trade. This often occurred on the active, as well as the inactive, side of the orderbook. This means HFT is increasingly willing to replace that liquidity, which was pre– viously taken in the book, if there is more activity in the book and wider bid ask spreads (not just increase liquidity where it was not demanded). This evidence is consistent with liquidity following liquidity and liquidity being

This evidence is consistent with the concern of market participants that HFT, lacking the a±rmative obligation to provide liquidity which is the hallmark of traditional market making, will quickly withdraw from the market when it is convenient.
