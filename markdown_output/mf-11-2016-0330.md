## Abstract 

Purpose – Forecasting the future movement of yield curves contains valuable information for both academic and practical issues such as bonding pricing, portfolio management, and government policies. The purpose of this paper is to develop a dynamic factor approach that can provide more precise and consistent forecasting results under various yield curve dynamics. 

Design/methodology/approach – The paper develops a unified dynamic factor model based on Diebold and Li (2006) and Nelson and Siegel (1987) three-factor model to forecast the future movement yield curves. The authors apply the state-space model and the Kalman filter to estimate parameters and extract factors from the US yield curve data. 

Findings – The authors compare both in-sample and out-of-sample performance of the dynamic approach with various existing models in the literature, and find that the dynamic factor model produces the best in-sample fit, and it dominates existing models in medium- and long-horizon yield curve forecasting performance. 

Research limitations/implications – The authors find that the dynamic factor model and the Kalman filter technique should be used with caution when forecasting short maturity yields on a short time horizon, in which the Kalman filter is prone to trade off out-of-sample robustness to maintain its in-sample efficiency. Practical implications – Bond analysts and portfolio managers can use the dynamic approach to do a more accurate forecast of yield curve movements. 

Social implications – The enhanced forecasting approach also equips the government with a valuable tool in setting macroeconomic policies. 

Originality/value – The dynamic factor approach is original in capturing the level, slope, and curvature of yield curves in that the decay rate is set as a free parameter to be estimated from yield curve data, instead of setting it to be a fixed rate as in the existing literature. The difference range of estimated decay rate provides richer yield curve dynamics and is the key to stronger forecasting performance. 

Keywords Kalman filter, Dynamic factor model, Yield curve forecasting Paper type Research paper 

## 1. Introduction 

Yield curves on government bonds reflect overall economic conditions and government policies during the period, and contain useful information for bond pricing, portfolio management, and government policy formulation. Therefore, forecasting the movement of yield curves is important because it can provide valuable guidelines for both academic and practical issues[1]. The purpose of this paper is to develop a dynamic approach that can provide precise and consistent forecasting results under different situations.

In general, there are two popular ways of fitting historical yield curve data into term structure models – dynamic models and static models (Fabozzi, 2002). Dynamic models incorporate two approaches: no-arbitrage models and affine equilibrium models. Arbitrage-free models are built to be consistent with the observed yield curves of government bonds and bond prices. They are subsequently used to price other fixed-income products based on information that can be extracted from the models (Martellini et al., 2003). This approach focuses more on fitting to the yield curve at a given point of time. In contrast, affine equilibrium models focus on modeling the dynamics of the instantaneous rate under various assumptions about the risk premium. Prominent contributions in the dynamic models include Vasicek (1977), Cox et al. (1985), Hull and White (1990), Heath et al. (1992), and Duffie and Kan (1996). Unlike dynamic models, static models do not allow for the volatility of interest rates to be incorporated into the model. One widely applied static model is the Nelson and Siegel (1987) three-factor model which extracts the entire yield curve period to period into a three-dimension exponential framework.

However, despite the significant development in both term structure models and their estimation methods, there is still no consensus as to which models and estimation methods can provide more accurate forecasting results of yield curves. On the one hand, the no-arbitrage literature that involve yield curve forecasting generate mixed findings. Favero et al. (2012) indicate that no-arbitrage models are more useful to predict yield curves in shorter horizons. However, Moench (2008) argues that the arbitrage-free model can only deliver better forecasts at longer forecasting horizons. We find that the out-of-sample results of this group of term structure models are highly dependent on the chosen data set; that is, the forecasting ability of arbitrage-free models is poor due to the lack of robustness. The affine equilibrium models, on the other hand, also forecast poorly. Dai and Singleton (2000) and Duffee (2002) show that the equilibrium model cannot even beat the simple random walk process. Duffee (2002) also concludes that the failure of affine equilibrium models in forecasting future yields is driven by one of their key features – compensation for risk is a multiple of the variance of the risk. Thus, risk compensation cannot vary independently of interest rate volatility. Because of the poor forecasting performance of dynamic models, Diebold and Li (2006; hereafter DL) present a new approach that involves latent factors based on the Nelson and Siegel (1987) three-factor model. Results for both the in-sample and the out-of-sample tests are very encouraging. However, their method has an unavoidable shortcoming: they assume one crucial variable, the exponential decay rate, to be constant, which should be time varying by its own nature. A higher exponential decay rate means a faster decay speed of the slope and the curvature of yield curves, which means the curves will become flatter. Therefore, the exponential decay rate is an important dynamic factor related to the yield curves’ shape, which is a crucial indicator of economic conditions. 

In this study, we develop a unified dynamic factor approach based on DL’s method of forecasting the dynamic movement of yield curves for government bonds. We use a statespace model and the Kalman filter to extract the three factors of the yield curve and test the effects of the three factors on forecasting yield curves. We test the in-sample fit by using US yield curve data. We also compare the out-of-sample forecasting performance of our method against DL’s method as well as other popular models, including Fama-Bliss (1987), and Cochrane and Piazzesi (2005), random walk, slope regression, AR(1) and VAR(1). 

Our in-sample results show that our dynamic factor model can precisely fit the historical yield curve data. In comparison with the in-sample results of DL, our approach generates more accurate results due to the highly efficient performance of the Kalman filter. Our out-of-sample results show that unlike other various models, the dynamic approach can provide much improved forecasting results, especially for the long term forecasting periods. However, the forecasting results of short-term maturities for a short forecasting horizon are

The major contribution of this study is that we introduce a dynamic approach that can produce both impressive in-sample fitting and out-of-sample forecasting results. According to DL, the three time-varying parameters, β1t, β2t, and β3t, can be interpreted as three factors of short-term, medium-term, and long-term components. The long-term component is a constant that does not decay to zero in the limit. The medium-term curve is the only function within this model that starts out at zero and decays to zero. The short-term curve has the fastest decay of all functions within the model that decays monotonically to zero. All the yield curves with monotonic and humped shapes can be built by choosing different weights for the components. As a result, Nelson and Siegel (1987) conclude that a high correlation exists between the present value of a long-term bond implied by the fitted curves and the actual reported price of the bond.

The long-term factor, β1t, governs the yield curve level. Changing this factor will lead to changes in all yields equally. DL refer to the short-term component as the slope of the yield curve. This component will decay based on λ, the exponential decay rate, through time to maturity. A higher exponential decay rate will lead to faster decay. An increase in the shortterm factor, β2t, increases short yields more than long yields because the short rates load on β2t more heavily, thereby changing the slope of the yield curve. With regard to the mediumterm factor, β3t, changing it will change the medium-term yield curve, but will have little influence on the short-term or long-term curve. Thus, increasing β3t will increase the curvature of the yield curve.

We initialize the Kalman filter with λ as 0.0609, which is based on the choice of value from DL. However, they do not indicate why they fix λ to such a specific value. The window size is five years and both the starting date and the ending date move forward along with the forecasting process. ## 4.1 In-sample fit 

Panel A of Table II summarizes the statistics of β1t, β2t, and β3t estimates. The mean value of the level factor is 5.501. It has the middle level of persistence because its autoregression coefficient is between the slopes and curvatures. The mean value of the slope is −3.393 (the negative value means a positive slope), which is the most persistent due to the highest autoregression coefficient. The curvature factor is the least persistent factor and it is also the most variable one relative to its mean. Panel B of Table II shows the estimation of λ is 0.0397 and highly significant at any significance level. In our estimation, the value of λ is much less than the fixed value 0.0609 used in DL’s method. The difference between our λ results and the fixed value of DL is both economically and statistically significant. 

Figure 4 plots the average yield curves for both actual and model fitted data. The two sets are quite close in this plot. This means that our estimation process can provide a reasonably reliable and tractable fitting result based on the historical yield curve data. Figure 4 also presents three different yield curves for both actual data and model-based data by some selected dates. These yield curves have different shapes, including upward sloping, flat, humped, and inverted hump. Our dynamic method can capture the changing shapes and replicate a variety of yield curve shapes. 

Figure 5 plots β1, β2, and β3 over time. As expected, the level factor is more persistent than the other two factors. The slope factor, β2, is negative most of the time. Since the slope of yield curves is −β2, one can conclude that the yield curves mostly have a positive slope. For the curvature factor, a higher curvature means a more convex/concave yield curve. The yield curve should be flatter in the middle if the curvature is close to 0. We find that the slope factor and the curvature factor, for the period July 2005 to July 2007, to be close to 0. This is indicated by the almost flat line on the yield curves for that period. 

The decay factor λ extracted from the Kalman filter is significantly different from the fixed value set by DL. Thus, in Figure 5, we plot the three factors extracted by using both our λ and DL’s λ, as well as empirical proxies of these factors. The empirical proxy for the level is the ten-year yield, the slope is the difference between the three-month yield and ten-year yield, and the curvature is the difference between the twice of the two-year yield and the sum of the three-month and ten-year yield. We observe that both estimates from our dynamic approach and DL’s method have the same trend as empirical proxies.

## 4.2 Exponential decay rate (λ) 

Next, we assume that the exponential decay rate remains unchanged in the five-year estimation window. Then we extract all λ based on the moving window method. The λ date ranges from January 2006 through December 2012. DL consider λ as a constant and fix it to 0.0609 in the entire sample. The fixed value constrains the model implantability in a dynamic market where its components are variable overtime. Thus, relaxing this strong assumption and allowing λ to be time-varying, we extend and complement the DL’s approach. 

Panel C of Table II reports descriptive statistics of the estimated λ. The mean of λ is 0.0435, the maximum is 0.0563, and the minimum value is 0.0329. The standard error of λ is 0.0011, indicating a 95 percent confidence interval should be between 0.0375 and 0.0419. The fixed value of 0.0609 used by DL is much higher than the upper endpoint of the confidence interval – even higher than the maximum value of λ. If we estimate β1, β2, and β3 by fixing λ at 0.0609, then β1, β2, and β3 may not respectively be interpreted as the level, slope, and curvature of the yield curves for this period. Therefore, estimating all three betas and λ at the same time is necessary to produce in-sample fitting results. 

Figure 6 presents the dot plots of all the values of λ and their trendlines, depicting that λ tends to be downward sloping. This indicates a decreasing decay speed of both the slope and curvature. Therefore, compared with yield curves at the beginning of our sample, the yield curves at the later stages are steeper and have a higher degree of bending because of the lower decay rate.

In order to achieve its high efficiency, the Kalman filter simply chooses to ignore these short-term noises but attempts to maintain smoothness in the long-run forecast. Therefore, we suggest that caution be exercised when using Kalman filter in yield curve models. A simpler model with a lower estimation efficiency may produce relatively more robust forecasting results for short-term maturities and short-horizon forecasting. 

DL also indicate a similar reason why their method cannot beat simple models in the short-term forecasting period. According to DL, the performance of one-month-ahead forecasts using the Nelson and Siegel three-factor model does not dominate other models. However, the 12-months-ahead forecasts are much improved. DL argue that one-month-ahead forecasts can be influenced by many sources, and some of these sources cannot be picked by more sophisticated models. Their explanation is consistent with ours: the short-term prediction is largely driven by random noises. However, it is mid- and long-term predictions that are more important in both portfolio management and policy making as they can provide essential dynamic information about the macroeconomic environment.

## 5. Conclusion 

In this study, we develop a unified dynamic factor model based on DL three-factor model to forecast the dynamic movement of yield curves. We formulate a state-space model and estimate model parameters and extract dynamic factors from US yield curve data using the Kalman filter. We present both in-sample fit and out-of-sample forecast results and compare our approach with other competing models, including DL’s method and other well-known models.

We find that our dynamic method can efficiently extract three factors of yield curves with a time-varying decay rate. More importantly, our model not only provide the best in-sample fit, but it also produces superior out-of-sample forecasting results for the long forecasting horizons. However, it is worth noting that, despite its encouraging empirical results, the Kalman filter should be used with caution when applying it to short-term yield curve forecasting, in which the Kalman filter is prone to trade off out-of-sample robustness to maintain its in-sample efficiency. This is particularly alarming given that the Kalman filter has gained increasing popularity in term structure modeling. 

It is also worth noting that our dynamic method can extract the three factors and the exponential decay rate in a unified process so that we can overcome an estimation caveat of DL’s method which assumes a fixed exponential decay rate. We find that the fixed value of the exponential decay rate may not fit the data well for the sample. Instead, allowing the decay rate to be a free model parameter in our state-space framework drastically improves the model fit. Given that the exponential decay rate is a critical determinant of the shape of yield curves, it is not surprising that our dynamic approach demonstrates the superior in-sample fit and out-of-sample forecasting results for the long horizons. 

Our unified dynamic approach is simple to implement and it requires only the historical yield curve data. The dynamic feature of the decay rate λ adds an additional flexibility that adapts to changing market conditions, rendering our model a better forecasting ability for the long-term maturities. The term structure literature suggest that accurate long-term prediction of yield curves provide valuable macroeconomic information, including GDP growth (Ang et al., 2006), real activity, inflation, and the monetary policy instruments (Diebold et al., 2006). In addition, the yield curve is associated with the future economic activity, such as consumption and investment (Estrella and Hardouvelis, 1991). Therefore, our dynamic model and its Kalman filter implementation can be useful to investment managers for getting more accurate forecast of yield curve movements in the long run. In addition, our model can also be beneficial for macroeconomists and policy makers who can use our dynamic approach to extract valuable information about the macroeconomic environment.
