We construct a price impact model between stocks in a correlated market. For the price change of a given stock induced by the short-term liquidity of this stock itself and of the information about other stocks, we introduce a self- and a cross-impact function of the time lag. We model the average cross-response functions for individual stocks employing the impact functions of the time lag, the impact functions of traded volumes and the trade-sign correlators. We further quantify and interpret the price impacts of time lag in terms of temporary and permanent components. To support our model, we also analyze empirical data, in particular the memory properties of the sign self- and average cross-correlators. The relation between the average cross-responses and the traded volumes which are smaller than their average is of power-law form. 

Keywords: Price impact; market microstructure; statistical analysis. 

## 1. Introduction 

The price impact, i.e., price response to trades, quanti¯es the expected price change conditioned on initiating a trade with a given size and a given sign

(buy or sell) (Bouchaud et al., 2004; Moro et al., 2009; Farmer et al., 2013). A buyer-initiated trade is expected to push the price up, while a seller-initiated trade to drop the price down, as from an economic perspective an increase in the demand should raise the price, and an increase in supply should reduce the price. In the view of market microstructure, the price change corresponding to the buyer- or seller-initiated trades is due to market orders consuming limit orders up to a higher/lower price. Since the price impact has been studied numerously, it is not di±cult to ¯nd out its importance either in practice or in theory. In practice, to pro¯t from trades as much as possible, it is crucial to estimate the trading costs (Gatheral, 2010) caused by the price impact. A particular case is that to reduce the potential impact costs or conceal the real trading intention, traders prefer to submit small market orders or split extremely large orders into small ones. In theory, the market impact for a large split metaorder is estimated by the principle of \no dynamic arbitrage" or \no price manipulation" (Gatheral, 2010; Gatheral et al., 2012; Gatheral and Schied, 2013; Obizhaeva and Wang, 2013; Alfonsi and Acevedo, 2014; Alfonsi and Blanc, 2016). Speci¯cally, the market impact is based on the price impact of one transaction, where the functional form is to be found out. As the price change relates to the demand and supply of the stock (Hopman, 2007), the impact re°ects the shape of excess demand in economics. 

The price impact is mainly generated by three factors out of all possible ones, these are the short-term liquidity costs, the traded volumes and the e®ects due to information (Chan and Lakonishok, 1993; Farmer et al., 2004; Weber and Rosenow, 2006; Hopman, 2007; Farmer and Zamani, 2007; Joulin et al., 2008; Moro et al., 2009). In electronic markets, the market liquidity is self-organized, that is to say, any trader can choose to be liquidity taker or liquidity provider. Not only the market markers in the traditional sense provide liquidity. As liquidity providers, the traders post limit orders in the order book to sell or buy certain quantities at a minimum ask price or a maximum bid price, since request-for-quote is a way to trade in electronic markets. To execute a transaction immediately, the traders, as liquidity takers, consume liquidity by sending market orders to buy or sell certain quantities. The costs for making transactions without time delay are referred to as liquidity costs (Demsetz, 1968; Bartram et al., 2008). Although various relations between the price changes and the traded volumes were found (Potters and Bouchaud, 2003; Lillo et al., 2003), most studies (Farmer et al., 2004; Bouchaud et al., 2004; Weber and Rosenow, 2006; Joulin et al., 2008; Moro et al., 2009) demonstrate that large price changes are, on average,

not the result of large traded volumes. Rather, they are driven by the low density of limit orders stored in the order book. The less the liquidity, the larger is the gap of limit orders in the order book (Schmitt et al., 2012). The resulting heavy tails of the return distributions are one of the celebrated stylized facts and speci¯c features in price dynamics (Cont, 2001; Chordia et al., 2002; Bouchaud and Potters, 2003; Bouchaud et al., 2009; Chakraborti et al., 2011; T�oth et al., 2011; Eisler et al., 2012; Schmitt et al., 2012, 2013). The orders which are small due to the splitting are thereby responsible for the large price change on average (Bouchaud et al., 2004). The various e®ects of information always attract academic attention (Grossman and Stiglitz, 1976; Hasbrouck, 1988; Farmer and Zamani, 2007; Joulin et al., 2008; Gomes and Waelbroeck, 2015). Information can be classi¯ed as all the available public information, such as news, and the information which is often called private. The e±cient market hypothesis (EMH) (Fama, 1970) states that all available information is processed and encoded in the current prices, which would rule out any (statistical) arbitrage opportunity. Thus, if we leave aside, e.g., drastic political events, all the available public information in total does not typically generate large price change. This is consistent with the ¯nding that neither idiosyncratic news nor market-wide news can explain the frequency and amplitude of price jumps (Joulin et al., 2008). The price impact is more likely to be prompted by private information that is hidden in the trades and is then permanently incorporated in the new equilibrium price (Chan and Lakonishok, 1993). 

Previous studies have found that the impact is nonlinear in the traded volumes, described either by a power-law (Lillo et al., 2003; Farmer and Lillo, 2004) or by a square-root (Gabaix et al., 2003; Plerou et al., 2004) or by a logarithm (Potters and Bouchaud, 2003). The price impact is thus seen to be permanent and °uctuates in order size (Lillo and Farmer, 2004; Gerig, 2008). Nevertheless, another opinion proposed by Bouchaud et al. (2004) states that the price impact is neither linear in the volume nor permanent in time. It should rather be transient and ¯xed with order size. To be speci¯c, these authors constructed a price impact model depending on time, where a \bare" impact function is used to propagate the impact of a single trade. The bare impact function is due to the strong self-correlations of trade signs and, in turn, o®sets the ampli¯cation e®ect due to the time-accumulated trade sign correlation by decaying with time. The transient property of price impact is

Importantly, the price impact models mentioned above are all con¯ned to single stocks. That means the expected price change is only interpreted as a consequence of the trades in the same stock rather than in°uenced by the trading of other stocks. The two impact functions result from the short-term liquidity of the stock itself and the trading information of other stocks, respectively.

## 3.2. Properties of trade sign correlators 

For both, the self- and the cross-correlators of trade signs appearing in the response functions in Sec. 2, an empirical check of their memories is called for. For the long-memory sign correlation, a buy (sell) market order is more likely to be followed by other buy (sell) market orders. If new limit orders did not come in, a sequence of buy (sell) market orders would push up (down) the price continuously, leading to a persistent price change. In a normal market situation, new limit orders will arrive, so that the price change, on average, can persist only for a while and then will reverse. ## 5. Conclusion 

We put forward a price impact model for the average cross-response functions for individual stocks. It comprises two impact functions, i.e., a self-impact function and a cross-impact function. We introduced and studied three scenarios, namely cross-responses exclusively due to the trade-sign crosscorrelators (Scenario I), or to the trade-sign self-correlators (Scenario II), or to both (Scenario III), respectively. Thereby, we managed to greatly reduce the complexity of the problem, and facilitated the determination of the model parameters. 

In the empirical analysis we demonstrated that, for most stocks, the selfcorrelators and average cross-correlators of trade signs have a long memory, which provides a strong support for setting up the impact function of the time lag. The empirical analysis also revealed power-law relations between the average cross-responses and the traded volumes that are smaller than their average. The relations hold regardless of the reactive or proactive crossresponses and regardless of the traded volumes of the impacted or impacting stock. To further explore the parameter space of our model, we de¯ned the response kernels, which are the average cross-responses divided by the average impact functions of traded volumes. Our empirical analysis manifests that the smaller the volumes, the larger are the response kernels. 

As an example, we studied the price impacts of MSFT. The self-impact includes temporary and permanent components. The temporary component, as a decaying power-law function, is the result of short-term liquidity, while

the permanent component, approaching a constant, is due to private information. However, the permanent component is rather small compared to the temporary one. The cross-impacts, separated into a proactive impact and a reactive impact, only contain the temporary component. Here, the reactive impact is the price change of an individual stock induced by single trades of the other stocks, and the proactive impact is the average price change of di®erent stocks triggered by a single trade of an individual stock. The temporary component comes from the trading information transmitted across stocks. In our study, the trading information is limited to the trading directions, i.e., buy and sell, and traded volumes of impacting stocks. The interference of competing information weakens the in°uence of the trading information. Consequently, the cross-impacts are neither as strong as the selfimpact nor persistent permanently.
